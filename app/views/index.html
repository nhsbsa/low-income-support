Sprint 67

Experimental

Sending automated email (re-visited)

Hypothesis A

We believe giving applicants an additional option of supplying evidence by email will be a useful feature for all applicants who receive; wages from a job, universal credit or working tax credits as it will allow an additional route of providing evidence which meets their needs. This will decrease the amount of time it takes to receive an outcome by at least 1 day.

Hypothesis B

We believe sending an automated email to all applicants who receive; wages from a job, universal credit or working tax credits will be a useful feature for every assessor as it will save them time having to manually contact each applicant and request evidence. This will reduce the number of times an assessor has to request evidence by 100%.

What we changed:

  • If an applicant has entered an email address (96%), then we send them an email notification which details; what evidence is required, as well as how to send this by email or post.
  • If an applicant has not entered an email address (4%), we show a page which details; what evidence is required, as well as how to send this by post. They can also choose to send it via email if they give us an email address.
  • After a period of 30 minutes (2 minutes for prototype purposes), we re-direct to a unique timeout screen which details because they have been away from their device for 30 minutes, we have cleared their details and should they need reminding of what information we need from them, they should contact us and reference their digi-ref.
  • Added the ability to actually send a request for evidence email through GOV.UK Notify.

What we plan to test:

  • Can applicants successfully supply us with the correct evidence in a way that meets their needs?
  • If a user leaves the service for 30 minutes (2 minutes for prototype purposes), can they still get in contact with us to find out what evidence is needed?
  • What is the process when an applicant actually receives an email into their inbox?

What we found:

  • Participants were perfectly happy to supply the required evidence via email and even noted that if they couldn't they always had the option of sending it by post to the address mentioned in the email. One participant immediately hit 'Reply' and showed us what she would do.
  • This lead into further conversations around how it would be useful to be notified when a certificate is due to expire. On participant is currently adding expiry dates to her diary to make sure she doesn't forget - she suggested how useful it would be to get either an email or a text message when the date approaches.



Hypothesis C

We believe that hiding all cases that still require evidence will be a useful feature for every assessor as it will mean that assessors only register and deal with applications which have all of the information needed. This will speed up the assessment process and therefore mean more applicants receive decisions quicker.

What we changed:

  • When an assessor hits the 'Register' button, the API call will only register the next available case which doesn't have; wages from a job, universal credit or working tax credits.
  • The trigger for an assessor to pick up an earnings case will be receiving an email to the group inbox, or evidence arriving in the post.
  • At this point, the assessor would search for the digi-ref that has been provided, and manually register the case.

What we plan to test:

  • Does this workflow meet the assessors need of not looking at claims until they are ready to assess?

During our mini discovery on how to reduce the amount of time from 'Awareness to outcome' for applicants, we discovered that the Assessment team were increasingly spending more time dealing with applications where there was no response to the request for evidence.

Our theory is that this is because applying online has become so easy, that applicants are willing to 'give it a go' on the off chance that they receive help. Paper applications typically have more barriers to face before applying e.g. Purchasing a stamp, posting the application etc...

  • Is there an acceptable timeframe to 'close down' applications where applicants do not respond to requests for evidence, or can we provide an automated workflow where we remind applicants?

Sprint 64

Experimental

Asking for earnings information from applicants

Hypothesis A

We believe that capturing earnings information in LIS Apply, rather than asking for evidence, will be a useful feature for all applicants who receive wages from a job as it will mean that their claims are assessed quicker and therefore reduce the amount of time taken for the applicant to receive help with their health costs. This will also reduce decrease the cost of operations by XX%.

It takes time, effort and cost (on paper route) for users to send us evidence of their income. It also takes time and effort for assessors to decipher payslips in order to calculate weekly income, leading to an increased wait for the user to receive help.

We believe that by capturing this information from applicants directly, we will not only speed up outcomes, but reduce the overall cost of operations as well.

After sampling 100 payslips, we found that a high number (XX) included just basic information e.g. Gross Income, Tax, National Insurance and Work Pension figures.

What we changed:

  • We added in the ability to collect gross income, tax, national insurance and work pension details (if one is paid into).
  • For those complicated cases which receive additional additions / deductions e.g. company bonus, uniform allowance etc... we will still require payslips to be submitted as evidence.

What we plan to test:

  • Can users successfully submit information of their earnings?
  • Does it increase the risk of fraud and error from a business perspective?

Sprint 62

Experimental

Confirm email address and telephone number

Hypothesis A

We believe that getting applicants to confirm their telephone number and email address will be a useful feature for all applicants who wish to be reassured about their application progress as it will allow us to successfully contact them when there are updates to their application. This will decrease the number email bounces that will be received due to information being entered incorrectly.

During our work to reassure applicants that we have received their application, as well as reduce the number of multiple applications, we want to limit the number of email bounces received by getting applicants to check their email address after they have entered it.

Speaking to the assessment team, email bounces are a common occurance and are therefore a source of delay to applicants receiving help with their health costs.

One reason this happens for postal applicants is the assessment team not understanding the applicants handwriting.

What we changed:

  • After an applicant enters a telephone number or email address, we get them to confirm this is correct before proceeding.
  • We ensure that any applicant who does not wish to give us their telephone number or email address understands that this means that we can't update them on the progress of their application.

What we plan to test:

  • Does the solution mean a higher accuracy rate and therefore lower result in the number of email bounces?

Sprint 61

Experimental

State benefits improvement

Hypothesis A

We believe that amending the default view of the list of benefits to be expanded by default instead of collapsed will be a useful feature for all single & partner applicants who are in receipt of state benefits as it will allow them to successfully tell us what benefit they are on. This will decrease the number of times assessors need to contact applicants for further information by XX% and therefore increase the speed from awareness to outcome.

During our regular Assessor Feedback sessions, it was highlighted that Assessors were seeing a number of applicants say they received a state benefit, but when asked which one - they didn't select one from our list which which meant that they had to go back to clarify.

During research (PrB 60_2) we saw this first hand when during an initital conversation, the user told us they received 'ESA' but despite this option being available to them - they didn't select it.

We believe that this is because each person sees what type of benefit they are on very differently e.g. They saw 'ESA' as 'Sick Pay', whereas the section title said 'Disability, illness and carer benefits'.

What we changed:

  • Instead of the list of benefits being hidden under a section title that we have decided, we made it that each section was open by default.

What we plan to test:

  • Does the solution allow applicants to successfully tell us which benefit they receive.
  • This change also has the added benefit of being an improvement to users with learning disabilities - who during a recent session (11th April 2019) stated that longer lists were an improvement to them.

Sprint 60

Experimental

Council Tax

Hypothesis A

We believe that allowing applicants to enter a monthly council tax figure instead of a yearly one will be a useful feature for all applicants who pay council tax and live in a property that they own as it will enable them to enter an amount they know or can get access to quickly. This will decrease the number of times assessors need to contact applicants for the correct value by XX% and therefore increase the speed from awareness to outcome.

During our regular Assessor Feedback sessions, it was highlighted that Assessors were having to go back a number of times to clarify Council Tax figures from applicants.

During research (PrB 60_2) we saw this first hand with a user who immediately entered their monthly council tax figure, despite the question asking for how much they pay in total a year.

We believe this is because the monthly figure is a lot more accessible to them e.g. via online banking etc..., to get a yearly amount they either have to dig out the single letter they receive at the beginning of the year (if they've still kept it!) or attempt some maths in their head.

What we changed:

  • Added in the ability to choose the frequency of council payments e.g. 10 months, 12 months or a lump sum.
  • If a user selects 10 or 12 months, we ask for the monthly figure with some hint text to give us their first monthly payment if possible.
  • If a user selects a lump sum, we ask for the yearly figure.

What we plan to test:

  • Does the solution meet applicants needs as well as adhere to regulations.

Sprint 55, 56

Experimental

Save & Restore

Hypothesis A

We believe that allowing applicants to save and restore an incomplete application later will be a useful feature for all busy users who need help with their health costs as it will enable them to apply in a way that meets their needs and preferences. This will decrease the number of drop offs by XX%.

We researched other services that provided similar features e.g. NHS Pensions & Blue Badge Scheme, and they showed a similar user need:

Some days I can’t get out of bed, and there are times when I’m using the wheelchair and it takes everything to just feed myself for the day. People that are applying for this might not be quite as up and down but some kind of level, there is a chance they can’t do this all in one go.

What we changed:

  • Added in the ability to save an application at key points. For example, at the end of the 'Before You Start' journey, as well as on the main task list page.
  • During this save process we ask them to provide a memorable word and email address. The details of how to resume an application will then be emailed to them.
  • Added in a gateway page which asks for memorable word, reference number and applicants date of birth. All 3 combined will grant access to a previous application.
  • After 3 failed attempts to resume an application, an error page will be shown which tells them we are unable to confirm their identity, and they are invited to start a new application.

What we plan to test:

  • Does the solution meet applicants needs as well as adhere to security guidelines.

What we found:

  • Users would expect to be able to save an application at any point throughout the application and if they were to return, the task list should say 'In Progress'.
  • The Blue Badge scheme went one step further than NHS Pensions and used Gov Notify to send a 7 digit code to the email address or phone number that was collected during the application to allow an applicant to resume. One potential issue is that these fields aren't mandatory currently in LIS.
  • Security were concerned that there was a risk that somebody might input their email address / mobile number incorrectly and this could potentially allow someone else access to their application.



Hypothesis B

We believe that allowing applicants to save an application at any time, as well as return to it at a time convenient to them will be a useful feature for all busy users who need help with their health costs as it will enable them to apply in a way that meets their needs and preferences. This will increase the number of completions by XX%.

What we changed:

  • Before a user can successfully save an application, they are asked to enter and verify an email address or mobile number by entering a 5 character code (to ensure they have entered the correct details). As well as this they are asked to enter a memorable word. As of 12th March 2019 only 25 users out of 4051 applications couldn't provide either an email address or mobile number.
  • Added in the ability to return to an application using a combination of seperate 5 character code sent to the email address or mobile phone number that they used to save the application, as well as their memorable word. Alpha research showed that pensioners didn't like jumping between different methods e.g. Computer > Mobile, so providing email as an option was important to this target group.
  • After a user has determined that they are eligible for help, they have the ability to save the application at each point of the service (End of eligibility checks, task list, individual sections etc...).
  • Research discovered that certain applicants didn't just want to save their application and return later, they wanted to save their application and continue. Because of this, once saved, the applicant can also choose to continue as well.

What we found:

  • ...


Hypothesis C

We believe that allowing applicants to save an application at any time, as well as return to it at a time convenient to them will be a useful feature for all busy users who need help with their health costs as it will enable them to apply in a way that meets their needs and preferences. This will increase the number of completions by XX%.

What we changed:

  • To conform to GDS standards, we've updated all buttons from 'Next' to 'Continue'.
  • After an application is saved for the first time, we change the primary action from 'Continue' to 'Save and continue' to signify that the service is now saving a user’s information each time they proceed.
  • We brought forward the 'Who are you applying for' question to the very front of the application to ensure that we can be as clear as possible to users who are applying on behalf of someone else, that if they choose to 'Save the application' for later - that we need the APPLICANT's email / mobile details and not theirs.

Sprint 52, 53 & 54

Experimental

Submitting Evidence of Earnings (Single & Partner Applicants)

Hypothesis A

We believe adding the ability to provide evidence by email OR post will be a useful feature for users who are required to submit earnings evidence as it will allow them a digital route of providing evidence other than post. This will increase the number of users allowed to claim digitally by XX%

What we changed:

  • If a user enters an email address, we give them the option of whether they would like to submit their evidence by email or post, otherwise we ask them to submit it by post. Available for both single and partner applicants.

What we plan to test:

  • Whether applicants (single & partner) can successfully choose how they wish to supply evidence of their income (if required).

What we found:

  • A few applicants who we researched probably could supply evidence by email if they needed to, but the majority still liked the perceived security of sending in evidence by post (even the demographics who we expected to be more 'tech-savy' e.g. Students). We plan on extending this research before commiting to email fully as a viable option.



Hypothesis B

We believe showing what specific evidence is required from applicants on the end end page will be a useful feature for all users who receive earnings as it will allow them to submit these as quickly as possible and not provide unnecessary delay to any potential treatment. This will decrease the number of times the assessors have to go back to applicants by XX%.

What we changed:

  • If an applicant needs to provide evidence, instead of waiting for an assessor to get in touch, we tell the claimant up front what information we need from them and allow themselves to send this in digitally or by post. This brings digital back on a par with the paper form.
  • We've also added in the functionality to save what evidence is required (and their instructions) as a PDF or print it instead.

What we plan to test:

  • Whether applicants realise the end page cannot be returned to and what their preferred method of saving this is.
  • Whether applicants (single & partner) can successfully supply the correct evidence of their income first time.

What we found:

  • Users found that having to make a choice of which method they wished to supply evidence, without knowing what was actually required, was very difficult.
  • The majority of users were confused by the huge volume of information on both the email & post screens. Suggestions included breaking it down in a chronological order of what to do next.
  • Users needed further clarification to include the digi-ref in the subject line of the email.
  • Users very concerned about being unable to return to the page. Because of this, in the future, users would like the additional option of having the list of evidence emailed to themselves as well.




Known Issues

  • Prototype only includes new / amended screens and is not intended to be a full representation of the entire service.
  • No error messages in the prototype.
  • Uses dummy data on final 'check your answer' screens.
